# Chapter 4: Graphite — China's Dominant Position

## 4.1 Introduction

Natural graphite is the foundational material of the lithium-ion battery anode. Every commercially produced lithium-ion cell — in electric vehicles, grid storage, consumer electronics, and defence applications — contains a graphite anode, and no commercially viable short-run substitute exists. Synthetic graphite can replace natural graphite at three to five times the cost and with different electrochemical properties requiring cell redesign; silicon anodes are an emerging complement but not a near-term replacement at scale. This material dependency, combined with China's near-total control of both mining and processing, makes graphite the highest structural-vulnerability mineral in the US critical minerals portfolio.

This chapter presents a full causal analysis of graphite across two episodes separated by fourteen years: the 2008 pre-EV export quota cycle and the 2022 EV-era export licence system. The two episodes together constitute the core empirical contribution of this thesis — the same commodity, the same dominant supplier, the same causal model, two structurally distinct regimes. The structural break between episodes is captured entirely in the calibrated parameters, and the parameters themselves are independently identifiable from non-price evidence, satisfying the second line of validation set out in the methodology.

The chapter proceeds as follows: Section 4.2 establishes the market context and US strategic exposure. Section 4.3 presents the causal knowledge graph for graphite showing how HippoRAG-retrieved document evidence maps to the causal structure. Section 4.4 covers Episode 1 (2008). Section 4.5 covers Episode 2 (2022). Section 4.6 presents the L3 duration analysis — how long prices remain elevated after the restriction ends. Section 4.7 examines trade flow circumvention. Section 4.8 presents forward projections. Section 4.9 interprets the findings and their implications for the subsequent case study chapters.

## 4.2 Market Context and US Strategic Exposure

### 4.2.1 Supply Structure

China accounts for approximately 90% of global natural graphite exports by volume (USGS Mineral Commodity Summaries 2024). More critically, China controls the dominant share of downstream anode processing: battery-grade spherical graphite requires purification to above 99.95% carbon purity, and this processing is overwhelmingly performed by Chinese facilities. As of 2022, China's share of battery-grade anode material processing was approximately 95% — the highest processing concentration of any critical mineral in this study.

The effective control at each stage of the supply chain, computed from CEPII bilateral trade data (PRODUCES relationship) and USGS processing data (PROCESSES relationship) via the causal knowledge graph, is:

| Year | China PRODUCES share | China PROCESSES share | Effective control | Binding stage |
|------|---------------------|----------------------|-------------------|---------------|
| 2008 | 56% | 65% | **65%** | Processing |
| 2015 | 46% | 80% | **80%** | Processing |
| 2022 | 32% | **95%** | **95%** | Processing |

The PRODUCES share declines over time as other producers (Mozambique, Tanzania, Brazil) grow their export share. The PROCESSES share rises sharply as Chinese anode manufacturers invested heavily in processing capacity to capture battery supply chain value. The binding constraint — the stage that determines effective control — is processing throughout the entire period, and becomes nearly absolute by 2022. A country's raw graphite mine output share is therefore a poor indicator of US supply vulnerability; the processing share is the operative metric.

### 4.2.2 US Demand and Strategic Exposure

US annual consumption of natural graphite was approximately 90,000 tonnes in 2022 (USGS MCS 2024), with battery manufacturers comprising the fastest-growing demand segment. The United States has produced no domestic natural graphite since approximately 1990 and holds no strategic reserve. Unlike cobalt or lithium, for which non-Chinese supply chains have at least partial capacity, anode-grade graphite processing outside China is negligible. The US battery industry has no bridge technology for a supply disruption and no strategic buffer.

This creates the structural conditions for maximum vulnerability: single-source processing, no domestic alternative, no strategic reserve, inelastic demand from a growing sector, and a dominant supplier with both the motive and mechanism to restrict.

## 4.4 Episode 1: The 2008 Export Quota (Pre-EV Regime)

### 4.4.1 Historical Context

China progressively tightened graphite export quotas from 2009 through 2012, with the most restrictive measures imposed in 2010–2011. The episode has four overlapping shock components: (1) a pre-restriction demand surge (2008: +46% demand shock from steel sector growth and early battery industry expansion); (2) the Global Financial Crisis demand collapse (2009: −40% macro demand shock); (3) the export quota implementation (2010–2011: 35% quota reduction); and (4) a simultaneous capex freeze (50% reduction in investment target) as investors withdrew from graphite projects during the GFC period.

The market context is pre-EV: graphite demand in this period was primarily industrial — electrodes for electric arc furnaces, lubricants, refractories, and early lithium-ion cell manufacturing for consumer electronics. Battery demand existed but had not yet made graphite strategically inelastic. The US imported graphite primarily for steel and refractory applications.

This OOS degradation is itself a finding: the structural break is real, regime-specific, and correctly identified by the calibration.

## 4.5 Episode 2: The 2022 Export Licence (EV Restriction Regime)

### 4.5.1 Historical Context

China's Ministry of Commerce implemented a graphite export licence system in October 2023, requiring exporters to obtain individual permits for spherical graphite and graphite electrodes. The stated rationale was environmental protection and national security. The operational effect was a supply restriction: processing licence approvals took weeks to months, introducing delays equivalent to a de facto quota, and the licence mechanism provided China with a legal framework for selective denial without a formal quantity cap.

The 2022 episode covers the period from the EV-driven demand surge (2021–2022) through the export licence implementation and subsequent market adjustment (2023–2024). Between these years, the graphite market had structurally transformed: EV battery anodes had become the dominant demand segment, LFP (lithium iron phosphate) chemistry had grown as a lower-cost battery option with reduced graphite per kWh, and China's anode processing share had reached approximately 95% (USGS PROCESSES data, per KG). These three forces — demand surge, demand composition shift, and supply restriction — operate simultaneously in this episode, making it the most structurally complex case in the dataset.

### 4.5.2 Calibrated Parameters

From `outputs/predictability_run.txt`:

| Parameter | Value | Economic interpretation |
|-----------|-------|------------------------|
| α_P | 2.615 | Fast price adjustment; EV demand inelastic, concentrated supply amplifies shocks |
| η_D | −0.777 | High demand elasticity relative to 2008; LFP/Si-anode substitution possible over 2–3 yr |
| τ_K | 7.830 yr | Mine development cycle; stable from 2008 episode (geological constant) |
| g | 0.973/yr | Near-flat: EV demand growth offset by LFP adoption reducing graphite per kWh |

The α_P = 2.615 is the largest price adjustment parameter in the dataset, joint first with cobalt_2016. The product |α_P × η_D| = 2.031 exceeds 1.0, which is the stability boundary for the ODE's price feedback loop — in an unrestricted simulation the ODE exhibits amplifying dynamics. This is stabilised for multi-year forward projections by capping α_P at 0.9/|η_D| = 1.158 (Euler stabilisation). The crisis-calibrated α_P = 2.615 correctly captures the short-run 2022–2023 price dynamics; the stabilised value is used for forward scenario projections.

The α_P regime shift from 0.500 (2008) to 2.615 (2022) is the central empirical finding of this chapter. The magnitude of the shift — 5× over fourteen years — is not an artefact of model choice: it is structurally identified by the independent evidence in Table 4.1 (Section 4.9 below) and is corroborated by Benchmark Mineral Intelligence's battery demand data and China's processing market share trajectory.

All three directional calls correct. The Spearman ρ = 0.800 (vs 1.000 for the 2008 episode) reflects a genuine model limitation: the 2022 episode includes a large positive price move in 2022 (+49.5% CEPII) that the model under-predicts (+22.4%). This reflects two forces the annual ODE cannot capture: (a) speculative buying ahead of the anticipated October 2023 licence announcement, which pulled 2023 price increases forward into 2022; and (b) the 1-year lag in the ODE's price update mechanism — the 2022 demand surge only fully propagates into the price equation in 2023, one year later than the CEPII data shows. MagR = 3.04 indicates the model over-predicts the magnitude of the 2023–2024 price decline; the CEPII series stays higher (1.27×) than the model predicts (0.70×) at end-2024, likely because actual graphite prices were partially supported by continued producer inventory management and delayed LFP adoption in premium EVs.

The L3 counterfactual causal effect is **+111.5 pp**: the export licence system caused graphite prices to be 111.5 percentage points higher than the counterfactual world without the licence requirement. This is the largest causal effect in the dataset and is statistically distinguishable from zero (null distribution test: p < 0.01 at 1,000 bootstrapped permutations of the shock assignment; Chapter 4 methodology).

### 4.5.4 Out-of-Sample Transfer

The graphite_2022 parameters, applied to the graphite_2008 episode (OOS cross-epoch transfer), achieve **DA = 0.333**. This is the lowest OOS score in the clean validation set. The 2022 parameters (α_P = 2.615, η_D = −0.777) impose EV-era dynamics on the 2008 pre-EV market: the model over-predicts price amplification during the GFC period because the 2022 high-α_P regime expects sharper price moves than the pre-EV market actually produced. This result is the mirror image of the 2008→2022 transfer: both cross-epoch transfers confirm that the structural break is genuine and bidirectional — neither epoch's parameters generalise to the other.

The asymmetry (2022→2008 DA = 0.333 vs 2008→2022 DA = 0.600) is explained by the direction of the break: applying a high-amplification regime to a low-amplification episode fails more severely than applying a low-amplification regime to a high-amplification episode (which at least gets the direction right, if not the magnitude).

## 4.6 Pearl Layer 3: Duration Analysis

### 4.6.1 Why L3 Is Required

The question *"how long do prices remain elevated after the restriction ends?"* cannot be answered by L1 observation (confounded by contemporaneous demand and supply factors) or L2 intervention (which runs from a clean start without conditioning on the restriction-era damage that accumulated). Pearl Layer 3 addresses this by conditioning on the realised restriction-era trajectory — specifically, by abducting the unobserved structural residuals U_t from the CEPII price series and injecting them into the post-restriction simulation. This captures the carry-forward of restriction-era damage: inventory depletion, deferred investment, and speculative position unwinding that persist after the formal restriction ends.

### 4.6.2 Graphite 2022: L3 Results

From `outputs/duration_run.txt`:

**Abducted residuals U_t (CEPII vs ODE):**

| Year | U_t (raw) | U_t (endogeneity-corrected) | Note |
|------|-----------|----------------------------|------|
| 2021 | +0.000 | +0.000 | Baseline |
| 2022 | +0.434 | +0.434 | Pre-announcement speculation |
| 2023 | +0.223 | **+0.289** | Corrected: shock-active year |
| 2024 | +0.610 | **+0.145** | Corrected: shock-active year |

Restriction-year residuals (2023–2024) are corrected by linear interpolation to remove endogeneity: the raw U_t during active shock years incorporates restriction-driven dynamics that would be circular if used to evaluate the restriction's own effect. The corrected series is the preferred basis for the L3 normalisation lag.

**Normalisation lag by restriction end year T:**

| Restriction ends (T) | Norm year (full U_t) | Norm year (corrected) | Lag (corrected) |
|---------------------|---------------------|----------------------|-----------------|
| T = 2022 | 2023 | 2023 | +1 yr |
| T = 2023 | 2027 | 2024 | +4 yr / **+1 yr** |
| T = 2024 (factual) | **2027** | **2026** | **+3 yr / +2 yr** |
| T = 2025 | 2027 | 2026 | +2 yr / +1 yr |

*Factual restriction benchmark: T = 2024. The corrected lag of +2 yr is the preferred estimate; the uncorrected +3 yr provides an upper bound.*

The L3 finding is that graphite prices remain elevated for **2–3 years after the restriction ends**, even under an immediate removal scenario. This lag is driven by τ_K = 7.83 yr: the market cannot rebuild anode supply chain inventory to target levels faster than the underlying mine and processing capacity allows, and this capacity constraint persists regardless of when the formal policy restriction is lifted.

### 4.6.3 Policy Implication

The operational stockpile recommendation derived from the L3 analysis is:

1. Begin strategic reserve drawdown at restriction onset (not at restriction end)
2. Sustain drawdown for τ_K/2 ≈ **4 years** beyond restriction end
3. Replenish only once prices return within 10% of the no-restriction baseline

For the actual October 2023 licence implementation (T = 2024 factual end): maintain drawdown through approximately 2028. A stockpile designed only to cover the restriction period itself would be exhausted precisely when post-restriction inventory rebuilding pressure is keeping prices elevated.

## 4.7 Trade Flow Circumvention

### 4.7.1 Can Third-Country Routing Substitute for Direct Supply?

The transshipment analysis examines whether the restriction can be circumvented by purchasing Chinese graphite through intermediary countries — re-exporting it under non-Chinese trade designations. CEPII bilateral flow data (2019–2024) is used to detect post-restriction increases in trade flows that deviate from the pre-restriction trend.

**Pre-restriction graphite flows (2022 baseline):**

| Route | Tonnes/yr | Share of China exports |
|-------|-----------|----------------------|
| China → USA (direct) | 40,710 | 18.9% |
| China → South Korea | 46,986 | 21.8% |
| China → Japan | 35,473 | 16.5% |
| China → Germany | 22,054 | 10.3% |
| China → Canada → USA | 2,285 | 1.1% |

### 4.7.2 Results

Post-2023 statistical detection identifies one significant rerouting hub: Poland, with a +186% increase in Chinese graphite inflows post-restriction (p = 0.065, borderline significant at 5% threshold). The estimated circumvention rate — the fraction of the nominal restriction volume that reaches the US through non-direct channels — is **6%** (95% CI: [0%, 8.8%]).

South Korea and Japan, which receive large volumes of Chinese graphite, are not circumvention hubs: they add genuine manufacturing value (anode processing, cell assembly) rather than simply re-exporting raw material.

### 4.7.3 Interpretation

China's export licence system is approximately **94% effective** against the US. The 40,710 t/yr direct flow is effectively severed by the restriction; the circumvention channel recovers at most approximately 2,400 t/yr. This is insufficient to bridge a supply gap for even one major US battery manufacturing facility. Third-country routing is not a viable substitute for strategic reserve or domestic supply development.

The 6% circumvention figure also sets an upper bound for any allied supply response in the near term: the processing concentration (95% China) means that graphite sourced from Mozambique or Tanzania still requires Chinese anode purification before it is battery-grade. Until non-Chinese anode processing capacity exists at scale, the effective substitution rate is constrained below the mining-level diversification figures would suggest.

## 4.8 Forward Projection: L2 Do-Calculus Scenarios

### 4.8.1 Scenario Design

The forward projection applies Pearl Layer 2 do-calculus to the 2025 baseline: *do(export\_restriction = magnitude)* for a specified duration, starting from the 2024 calibrated state. Three scenarios are evaluated:

| Scenario | Restriction magnitude | Duration | Years active |
|----------|----------------------|----------|--------------|
| MILD_BAN | 30% | 2 years | 2025–2026 |
| FULL_BAN | 30% | 3 years | 2025–2027 |
| SEVERE_BAN | 50% | 4 years | 2025–2028 |

These are L2 interventions, not L3 counterfactuals: they evaluate *what would happen if* the restriction were imposed, without conditioning on a realised trajectory. The Euler-stabilised α_P = 1.158 (capped from 2.615) is used throughout for multi-year numerical stability; the stabilised value is the structural long-run price adjustment speed and is appropriate for planning horizons beyond 2 years.

### 4.8.2 Results

| Baseline | 1.000 | 1.000 | 0.946 | 0.916 | 0.884 | 0.856 | 0.829 | 0.803 | 1.000 | 2025 | 2025 | — |

### 4.8.3 Interpretation

Under FULL_BAN (the policy-relevant benchmark: a 30% restriction sustained for 3 years, consistent with the 2023–2024 licence system pattern), graphite prices peak at **1.59× the 2024 baseline** in 2026 and do not normalise until 2030 — three years after the restriction ends in 2027. This 3-year post-restriction lag is consistent with the L3 duration analysis (Section 4.6) and is structurally determined by τ_K = 7.83 yr.

The SEVERE_BAN scenario (50%, 4 years) peaks at **2.02× baseline** — a doubling of graphite prices that would make battery cell costs uneconomic at current volumes and would directly constrain US EV production timelines. The earlier normalisation under SEVERE_BAN vs. FULL_BAN (2029 vs. 2030) reflects the oscillatory dynamics of the ODE at high restriction magnitudes: a more severe initial restriction depletes inventory faster, triggering an earlier (but sharper) price reversal once the restriction ends.

The baseline projection (no new restriction) shows gradual decline to 0.80× 2024 levels by 2031. This reflects g = 0.973/yr — near-flat demand growth as LFP adoption moderates the per-kWh graphite content growth — combined with the absence of supply constraints. Under baseline conditions the graphite market is not tight; the vulnerability is entirely conditional on a restriction event.

## 4.9 Structural Parameter Identification

The causal engine's parameters are calibrated by differential evolution to maximise directional accuracy and Spearman ρ against CEPII data. This section provides the second line of validation: each parameter is identified against non-price evidence that was never used in calibration. The consistency of calibrated values with independent evidence confirms that the model is not simply curve-fitting to price history but is recovering structural economic quantities.

| Parameter | graphite_2008 | graphite_2022 | Independent identification |
|-----------|--------------|--------------|---------------------------|
| α_P | 0.500 | **2.615** | Pre-EV: industrial commodity, multiple-grade substitution. EV-era: Benchmark Mineral Intelligence battery demand share; China anode HHI; literature on inelastic battery demand (Helveston et al. 2022) |
| η_D | −0.073 | −0.777 | 2008: diverse industrial end-uses with grade flexibility. 2022: battery-grade anode has no short-run substitute at cell level; 2–3 yr substitution horizon for LFP/Si-anode transition |
| τ_K | 8.276 yr | 7.830 yr | USGS graphite project development timelines: Tanzania (Mahenge: 9 yr), Mozambique (Balama: 7 yr), Ethiopia (Oromia: 8 yr); consistent across both episodes confirming geological constant |
| g | 1.132/yr | 0.973/yr | 2008: steel+battery combined demand trajectory (IEA). 2022: EV volumes rising but LFP reducing graphite content per kWh (IEA Global EV Outlook 2023) |
| I₀ | ~0 | ~0 | No US natural graphite production since 1990; no NDS holdings (USGS MCS 2020–2023); processor inventory ≈ 60–90 days |

The α_P regime shift from 0.500 to 2.615 is the single most informative result. It is not a modelling artefact — it is recovered independently by the calibration on two separate episodes and is corroborated by the structural transformation of the graphite market between 2008 and 2022: the entry of EV battery anodes as the dominant demand segment converted graphite from an industrial commodity with substitution options into a battery input with near-zero short-run substitutability. The causal model detects this structural break from price and quantity data alone; the independent evidence confirms it.

## 4.10 Chapter Summary

The graphite case study establishes five findings that carry forward to the subsequent case study chapters:

1. **α_P = 2.615 (EV era) vs 0.500 (pre-EV)**: the largest regime shift in the dataset. A 5× increase in price adjustment speed, structurally identified by battery demand data, represents the fingerprint of a commodity transitioning from industrial input to strategic constraint. The threshold of α_P ≥ 1.5 — crossed by graphite between 2008 and 2022 — is proposed as the early warning signal for strategic vulnerability transition.

2. **L3 causal effect: +111.5 pp (2022) vs +58.1 pp (2008)**. The EV-era restriction produces nearly double the causal price effect of the pre-EV restriction, driven by higher α_P and lower demand elasticity. The Pearl L3 framework recovers this difference by conditioning on the realised trajectory — an L1 or L2 analysis would conflate the structural amplification with contemporaneous demand and supply factors.

3. **τ_K = 7.8 yr is stable across regimes**. The geological constant does not change with demand regimes: new graphite mines take 7–9 years regardless of whether demand is driven by steel or batteries. This constancy validates the physical interpretation of τ_K and supports its use as an independent input to stockpile planning (stockpile duration = τ_K / 2 at minimum).

4. **6% circumvention rate: trade routing cannot substitute for supply**. The transshipment analysis confirms that China's export licence system is 94% effective. Third-country routing provides at most 2,400 t/yr of effective bypass — approximately 2.6% of US annual consumption. Graphite is unique among the minerals studied in that the processing concentration (95%) is downstream of mining, making diversification at the mine stage alone insufficient.

5. **I₀ ≈ 0 at both restriction onset dates**. The absence of a US strategic graphite reserve is documented and longstanding. The model's finding that cover* is breached within 12 months from a 6-month starting reserve translates to essentially immediate breach from the actual near-zero starting position. Both restrictions (2010, 2023) hit a market with no buffer. The forward scenario projections (Section 4.8) are therefore not worst-case analyses — they describe the base case for a country with no reserve.
